Numbers are beautiful!

Paul Erdős, one of the most prolific mathematicians of the 20th century, said "Why are numbers beautiful? It’s like asking why is Beethoven’s Ninth Symphony beautiful. If you don’t see why, someone can’t tell you. I know numbers are beautiful. If they aren’t beautiful, nothing is."

Some small numbers are insanely hard to compute!! In another famous and popular quote on the hardness of computing Ramsey numbers (the Ramsey number \(R(m,n)\) is the smallest number of vertices \(v\) such that every edge-coloring of the complete graph with \(v\) vertices using \(2\) colors (say Red and Blue) will contain a Red complete subgraph of order \(m\) or a Blue complete subgraph of order \(n\). By symmetry, \(R(m,n)=R(n,m)\)), Paul Erdős said "Suppose aliens invade the earth and threaten to obliterate it in a year's time unless human beings can find the Ramsey number for red five and blue five. We could marshal the world's best minds and fastest computers, and within a year we could probably calculate the value. If the aliens demanded the Ramsey number for red six and blue six, however, we would have no choice but to launch a preemptive attack." In case it makes you feel any better, the known bounds for \(R(5, 5)\) and \(R(6, 6)\) are, respectively, \(43\leqslant R(5,5) \leqslant 48\); and \(102\leqslant R(6, 6) \leqslant 165\). Frank Ramsey's proof of the existence of these numbers initiated the combinatorial theory which is now known as Ramsey Theory. Ramsey numbers can be generalized as \(R(n_1,n_2,\cdots n_c)\) which is the smallest number of vertices \(v\) such that every edge-coloring of the complete graph with \(v\) vertices will contain a complete subgraph of order \(n_i\) monochroatic in color \(i\) for some \(1\leqslant i\leqslant c\).
